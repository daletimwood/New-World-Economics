About

New World Economics is a living research document tracking the structural shift from transactional to value-based commercial models in enterprise technology.

The question at the centre of this work (how value is created, captured, and shared in enterprise technology) does not yield to a single analytical pass. The argument develops gradually, through a process closer to gestation than to modelling: material accumulates, patterns surface, and the sections are rewritten as understanding changes.

Rilke, advising a young poet, wrote: 'Live the questions now. Perhaps then, someday far in the future, you will gradually, without even noticing it, live your way into the answer.' (Letters to a Young Poet, Letter Four, 1903.) This publication proceeds on that premise. Its commercial equivalent is the distinction between leading and lagging signal. Conventional market intelligence is backward-looking by design: surveys, data, and analyst reports describe what has already become legible. This work attends to what is visible earlier: in the frustrations of practitioners at the edge, in experiments not yet named as trends. The aim is structured proximity to the frontier: to be present where the next thing is being invented before anyone has named it.

The work is principle-based and non-company-specific. Sources and references are collected at the foot of the page.

01

A Short History of Monetisation

Enterprise software has always been sold as a proxy for the value it creates, but the relationship between price and value has been tightening for thirty years, and the gap is now almost closed.

The earliest commercial software was sold as a capital asset. In the perpetual licence era, the transaction was simple and the logic was borrowed from manufacturing: a buyer paid once for a product, owned it, and the vendor moved on. Price was anchored to the cost of production and the vendor's required margin, with value assumed rather than measured. What it was worth to the buyer was not a question the contract asked.

Maintenance and support contracts arrived not as a concession to customers but as a mechanism for vendors to build recurring revenue on top of one-time sales. The annual maintenance fee, typically in the range of fifteen to twenty-five percent of the original licence, was presented as insurance against the future: access to patches, upgrades, and support. In practice, it was monetised access disguised as insurance.[1]

The SaaS model changed the terms of the relationship in ways that were not fully understood at the time. Subscription pricing made the ongoing nature of the commercial relationship explicit, and renewal became a forcing function. A customer who felt they were not receiving value could, in theory, simply not renew, and this possibility restructured the incentives on both sides of the table. The unit of pricing, however, remained disconnected from outcome: seats, users, and named accounts are measures of access, not of value delivered.

Usage-based pricing was the next step toward alignment. Consumption models that charged by the API call, the transaction, or the compute unit connected price to utilisation, at least. A customer who used the product heavily paid more. The relationship between price and consumption was, for the first time, direct. But utilisation is not value, and the gap between the two is where most of the commercial tension in enterprise software now lives.

A structural force is now visible that makes the transition from usage to outcome pricing not merely commercially desirable but economically necessary for a growing class of vendor. The cost architecture of AI-native products differs from SaaS in one precise way: inference costs accrue at the moment of work, not at the moment of access. In a classical SaaS deployment, the most active users are typically the most profitable, because the marginal cost of serving them approaches zero. In an AI-native product, the most active users are frequently the most expensive, because each interaction requires a compute cycle whose cost the vendor bears directly. The near-zero marginal cost assumption that underpinned seat-based pricing no longer holds. Pricing must migrate toward the unit of value delivered because the unit of cost has already moved there. The previous transitions were driven by buyer-side pressure; this one is being driven by the vendor's own cost structure.[20]

Where this leads

The logical endpoint of this thirty-year compression is a commercial model in which price is set by reference to the outcome the customer achieves, not the product the vendor delivers. That model exists today in isolated pockets of the enterprise software market.

Each transition in this history has followed a similar pattern. A new pricing model emerges that more accurately reflects the value exchange. Adoption is initially slow: it requires new measurement capabilities and a tolerance for commercial risk on both sides. Then it becomes standard faster than incumbents expect. By most accounts, the distance between SaaS as a curiosity and SaaS as the default was roughly a decade. There is no particular reason to think the next transition will take longer.

The arc may not stop at usage-based pricing. A further stage is now visible, in which the commercial exchange migrates into the moment of work itself. As AI agents acquire the ability to discover, evaluate, and activate capabilities at the point of need, the separation between procurement decision and deployment collapses. What previously required months of vendor evaluation, proof-of-concept, and internal approval can now be initiated within the environment where the work is being done. The purchasing cycle is not merely shortening. It is being relocated from a governance function into a production environment. If this pattern establishes itself, the next commercial model is not just more closely aligned with value: it is embedded in the delivery of value itself.[5]

02

The Shift, Observed

The clearest evidence that commercial models are changing is not in the pricing pages of software vendors. It is in the budget decisions of their customers, in the duration of their contracts, and in the structural compression of the margins that the previous era took for granted.

Enterprise buyers are arriving at renewal and expansion conversations with substantially more structured value data than they carried five years ago. FinOps functions, third-party benchmarking services, and internal spend analytics platforms have professionalised the buying process, closing the information gap that protected vendor pricing through most of the SaaS era. A seller who cannot articulate delivered economic value in the customer's own terms faces increasing pressure toward discounting.[2]

The same buyer is also signing for shorter periods. Contract duration, which served for two decades as the most reliable lagging indicator of vendor pricing power, is now compressing faster than the SaaS-era commercial model can adapt to. The behavioural logic is straightforward: in a market where the leading product in a category may be displaced inside a contract term, flexibility is worth more than the discount available for committing to term. Vendor revenue forecasts built on multi-year deals are losing one of their underlying assumptions.[8]

Contract duration in compression

Survey data from a 2026 cross-industry analysis of B2B software companies shows sub-one-year contracts for new logos rising from four percent of deals in 2023 to thirteen percent in 2026. Three-year contracts have fallen from twenty-eight to twenty-three percent over the same period. Sales cycles compressed by roughly a quarter across the second half of 2025, from twenty-five weeks to nineteen. A former chief revenue officer of one of the formative cloud data platforms describes the pattern as a rational response to a market in which leadership positions shift on a quarterly cadence: customers are not hesitant, they are correctly unwilling to lock into a configuration of tools that may be obsolete inside the contract term.

Source: SaaStr, reporting on ICONIQ State of Go-to-Market 2026, April 2026

The automation layer of enterprise software stacks is under particular pressure. Buyers are discovering that purpose-built AI agents can replicate the process automation tools sold as add-ons to core platforms, at a fraction of the cost and in a fraction of the time previously required. The implication is not the wholesale replacement of core systems, which carry compliance, integration, and auditability value that point solutions cannot easily replicate. It is the erosion of the module and add-on revenue that made those platforms profitable at scale. Vendors whose margins depend on the automation layer face a more immediate threat than those whose core value lies in data, workflow, or identity.

Signal from the market

A CIO at a data security firm generating over two billion dollars in annual revenue reports that early tests suggest he can cut spending on enterprise software automation tools in half by replacing them with AI agents built in-house. He is keeping the core platforms. He is not renewing the automation add-ons. The incumbents' response, that AI-built alternatives lack the compliance and auditability that regulated enterprises require, is a value argument, not a price one. It may be correct. But it is a harder argument to make at renewal than it was two years ago.

Source: practitioner account, March 2026

A structural repricing is under way in enterprise software, operating below the level of individual product decisions. Foundation model capabilities, accessible directly through API at variable and declining cost, have entered the procurement conversation as a new reference class against which existing tool investments are now being evaluated. The question that a sophisticated buyer brings to renewal is no longer only whether the product justifies the seat price: it is whether the functionality delivered could be approximated, at sufficient quality, using general-purpose AI infrastructure at a fraction of the cost. For vendors whose differentiated value sits in a narrow functional layer built on top of capabilities that are now widely accessible, this is not a negotiation. The new benchmark is not a competing product. It is general-purpose infrastructure.[10]

The new benchmark in a renewal conversation

A practitioner account published in April 2026 documents a real enterprise renewal in which a general-purpose AI integration replicated approximately ninety-five percent of a vendor's differentiated AI feature at fifteen percent of the equivalent token cost. The customer cut the renewal by forty-five percent. What is significant about this account is not the outcome but the mechanism: the ROI benchmark was set not by a competing product but by direct access to general-purpose AI infrastructure. The competitive reference class has expanded beyond the product category itself.

Source: OnlyCFO Newsletter, "Pricing Pressure Will Crush You," April 2026

The vendors furthest into the transition are showing a two-stage pattern that illuminates how the shift from efficiency to value capture unfolds. In stage one, AI is deployed internally to reduce operational costs: support cases handled at scale, staff reassigned, margin protected. The economics are clear and the results are measurable. In stage two, the same capability is turned outward, toward commercial opportunities that were previously uneconomical to pursue. Prospects too small, too dormant, or too geographically dispersed to justify the cost of human outreach become reachable. Stage two is where the transition from cost model to revenue model occurs.[6]

The two-phase pattern

One enterprise platform provider reports deploying AI agents to handle over three million customer conversations, generating one hundred million dollars in annualised cost savings and reducing case volume by eight percent. In the second phase, the same infrastructure was redirected toward dormant commercial opportunities previously considered uneconomical to pursue. The result was more than 3,200 influenced sales opportunities. The language used internally for this second phase is telling: it was described as turning "sawdust" into pipeline. Sawdust is the byproduct of work, previously valueless, now a feedstock for something new. The commercial logic of staying in stage one, once stage two has been demonstrated, is difficult to sustain.

Source: Fortune, April 2026
Two bets on the same transition

In 2024, two well-funded legal AI companies served comparable enterprise clients from similar competitive positions. One built infrastructure: GPU capacity, model fine-tuning, proprietary reasoning systems, and data flywheels that compounded with each inference. Every dollar went into compute. The other built a managed service: human reviewers assisted by AI tools, a delivery model whose costs scaled linearly with revenue. By late 2025, the infrastructure-first company had reached $190 million in annualised revenue at an $8 billion valuation. The managed-services company failed to close a funding round and entered a distressed sale. The divergence was not primarily about which product customers preferred. It was about which cost structure could compound. One had better unit economics per customer. The other had compounding physics. By the time the difference registered in the data, the gap was irreversible.

Source: Jacco van der Kooij, "Unstoppable Growth," Winning by Design, May 2026[20]
The cost of acquisition-led growth: aggregate evidence

Analysis of more than 72 public SaaS companies shows that customer acquisition cost roughly doubled and annual growth rates roughly halved between 2021 and 2026. Parallel deterioration is reported across major investor portfolios. The structural diagnosis emerging from practitioner research is that two decades of acquisition-led commercial models, built around generating pipeline from outside the customer base, have reached a ceiling. Growth loops, which generate the next wave of pipeline from inside an existing customer base through retention, expansion, and advocacy, do something economically different: unlike a funnel, whose costs scale with its output, a loop grows the system's capacity to generate pipeline with each customer who achieves measurable value. The connection to outcome pricing is direct. A loop cannot run on disconnected signals or unverified outcomes. It requires exactly the measurement infrastructure that outcome-based commercial models are designed to produce.

Source: Jacco van der Kooij, "How AI Turns Fragmented GTM Workflows Into Coordinated Growth Systems," Winning by Design Growth Institute, June 5, 2026[26]

Adoption data from the lower end of the enterprise market is showing a pattern that has preceded disruption in adjacent software categories. AI-native challengers with purpose-built commercial models, including pricing structures linked to customer outcomes and significantly lower total cost of entry, are taking measurable market share from incumbents among companies below one hundred million dollars in revenue. This is the cohort with the lowest switching costs and the least institutional inertia in procurement. It is typically where new commercial models prove themselves before moving upmarket.[3]

A second structural force is now visible on the supply side of the market. The unit economics of enterprise software are changing as AI inference, model routing, and vector database infrastructure enter the cost of goods sold. The classic SaaS margin profile, built on near-zero marginal cost of delivery, is being compressed for any product with meaningful AI content. Practitioner surveys are placing expected gross margins for AI-native products in the low fifties, against the seventy to eighty percent that defined the previous era. The commercial response is not to defend the old margin profile, which the underlying cost structure no longer supports. It is to redesign the pricing model around the layer where value and cost both accrue: a platform fee that prices access, combined with token or output-linked components that recover variable cost in proportion to consumption. Hybrid pricing of this form is now the primary commercial model for nearly half of B2B companies with embedded AI. The shift from transactional to value-based pricing is no longer only a buyer-led story. It is being forced by the economics on the vendor side as well.[9]

The margin floor is moving

A practitioner analysis of B2B software companies with embedded AI puts expected average gross margins at fifty-two percent for 2026, against the seventy-to-eighty percent that defined the SaaS era. The cause is structural: AI inference, model routing, and vector infrastructure are direct variable costs incurred at the moment of work, behaving more like a utility input than like the near-zero marginal cost of classical hosting. The pricing response taking shape is hybrid in form, combining a platform fee that prices access with token or output-linked components that price use. This is the commercial geometry of a model in which both cost and value occur in the act of consumption, and the contract has to recognise both. A founder building in the category puts the design principle directly: the platform layer prices the relationship; the token layer prices the work.

Source: practitioner analyses, ICONIQ 2026 State of AI, and founder commentary, April 2026
When token prices fall and bills explode

Token prices fell from sixty dollars per million to sixty cents over three years: a hundred-fold reduction. In the same period, AI bills expanded. A four-person startup incurred a $113,000 monthly bill from a single model provider. A technology company with large engineering teams burned through its entire annual AI budget within four months. The cause is structural rather than accidental. An AI agent does not consume tokens the way a single query does: it re-reads context, checks its own work, and generates intermediate steps, consuming sixty to one hundred and forty times the tokens of a direct reply. Token pricing, like seat pricing before it, is a proxy for value that breaks down at the scale of agent-driven work. The practitioner conclusion already emerging is direct: track cost per completed task, not per seat and not per token. The unit of account must become the unit of value delivered.

Source: "Are AI Employees More Expensive Than Humans?" lex.substack.com, reported in TLDR Founders, 3 July 2026[27]

The commercial response to this new cost structure is not uniform, and the most instructive divergence is between vendors who have moved outcome pricing from commercial thesis to live contract and those who have not. The architecture emerging from the earliest AI-native deployments suggests a three-layer approach that addresses the specific failure modes of the classical SaaS freemium model in an AI context. The first layer gates intensity rather than capability, charging for volume, context length, and compute priority rather than for access to features. The second layer gates outcomes rather than access: the customer pays for confirmed value delivered, not for the opportunity to attempt delivery. The third layer gates the heaviest compute modalities, whose cost of delivery at scale is prohibitive regardless of pricing tier. What distinguishes this architecture from its predecessors is not the mechanics but the principle: the paywall sits on the unit of work the buyer cares about, not on the feature that produces it.[15]

The outcome layer in practice

One AI-native customer service platform is currently priced at $0.99 per resolved support ticket. The service is free to attempt; payment is triggered only when the customer confirms the problem was actually fixed. This is outcome-based pricing in live commercial deployment, operating at scale, not in pilot or strategic account structure. Practitioners working on AI pricing are naming the structural reason this approach matters: the classical SaaS freemium model creates what they are calling an AI freemium paradox. If the free tier is powerful enough to generate genuine use, users question why they should pay. If it is too restricted to demonstrate the product's capability, conversion never occurs. The outcome layer bypasses both: the customer pays for what was useful, not for access to what might be useful. The distinction is not a pricing tweak. It is a different theory of what is being sold.

Source: Arnon Shimoni, "Pricing is a Product Surface," arnon.dk, 5 May 2026; citing practitioner analysis of Google AI subscriptions and Intercom Fin commercial structure

What distinguishes the present moment from earlier phases of this transition is the movement of the outcome model from AI-native challengers into the incumbent cohort. When a major incumbent in a category publicly retires its deflection-based pricing and restructures its commercial model around verified resolution, it does two things at once: it confirms that the architecture is viable at enterprise scale, not only in the conditions favourable to a challenger, and it shifts the competitive pressure onto others in the category who have not yet moved. A model that was once an experiment at the edge is becoming a commercial baseline that others are expected to explain their distance from.[19]

Incumbents commit to outcome billing

At its annual customer conference in May 2026, one of the largest enterprise customer service platforms announced that its AI agents would be priced solely on verifiably resolved outcomes, retiring the deflection-based pricing that has defined the category for two decades. Verification is two-stage: the resolving AI agent and an independent AI evaluation model both confirm the resolution; routine exchanges are excluded. The platform underpinning the commitment has been trained on roughly twenty billion customer service interactions. The company's chief executive stated publicly that AI agents would handle more customer service interactions than humans during the current year, with a five-year projection in which the majority of such interactions would be AI-to-AI. This is not a pilot or a commercial experiment at the margin. It is a business-model commitment, at scale, by a major incumbent. When AI-native challengers first adopted outcome-based pricing, the argument against generalising the pattern was that the model was too complex to operate at enterprise scale. That argument is now harder to make.

Source: Zendesk, Relate 2026 conference and newsroom announcement, May 19, 2026

The transition from practitioner experiment to enterprise norm is now measurable in adoption data for the first time. A cross-industry survey of enterprise software decision makers conducted in the first half of 2026 finds that 18.7% of enterprises now use outcome-based pricing tied to agreed-upon metrics. This approaches parity, for the first time, with per-user, per-month models at 17.1%. More than half of buyers identify pricing model as a key purchase decision criterion. This is the first empirical indicator that outcome-based pricing has crossed from pioneer adoption into competitive standard: a rate that approaches parity with the incumbent model is not an edge case that requires both parties to take an unusual commercial risk. It is a market segment large enough to anchor a procurement conversation.[25]

Pay-per-resolution at scale: the largest incumbent commits

In June 2026, the largest vendor in the enterprise customer relationship management category launched a customer service agent priced at two dollars per fully resolved interaction, deployed initially on its own help portal, where the model achieved a seventy percent resolution rate across four point three million inquiries. Payment is triggered only when the issue is resolved autonomously from start to finish; interactions where the customer requests a human transfer, or leaves without resolution, are not charged. The vendor describes the design principle as "pay only for success." Two structural observations follow. First, the vendor has tested the model against its own demand before commercialising it: the verification mechanism carries the vendor's own operational credibility before it reaches the buyer's procurement table. Second, the entry of the largest incumbent in the category to pay-per-resolution at this price point changes the competitive reference class for every other vendor in the resolution space. Smaller and mid-market platforms that have operated outcome-based models since 2022 will now be positioned, from the buyer's perspective, as precursors to an enterprise-scale model rather than as innovators in a niche one. The analyst commentary accompanying the announcement identifies governance, not capability, as the gating factor for scale: the question of whether enterprises will trust the verification mechanism is the primary constraint on how fast the model spreads.

Source: Salesforce newsroom, Agentforce Help Agent announcement, June 25, 2026; Futurum Group, Kirkpatrick, K., "Salesforce's Agentforce Help Agent Bets on Pay-Per-Resolution: Will Enterprises Trust the Model?" June 25, 2026[25]

A further dimension of this transition is not yet visible in aggregate market data, and for structural rather than statistical reasons. Under annual contracts, the gap between when a buyer decides not to renew and when that decision registers in renewal data is the length of the remaining contract term. Practitioner accounts from April 2026 suggest that ninety percent or more of AI-driven churn is absent from market statistics for a structural reason: the decisions to leave have already been made, but the contracts under which those buyers are held have not yet expired. The buyers who will not renew have already stopped investing attention in those products, redirected budget to AI infrastructure, and begun the parallel experiments that will replace them. The churn signal is present; it is running approximately one contract cycle ahead of the data.[12]

The year of churn: mechanisms

A practitioner CFO writing in April 2026 describes ten specific mechanisms by which software contracts are being cut in 2026. The structural observation that sits above them is this: ninety percent or more of AI-driven churn has not yet appeared in renewal data because the relevant contracts have not yet expired. The decisions are made; they are not yet visible. The specific mechanisms include AI budget trade-offs, in which teams must cut existing tool spend to fund token budgets; overlap consolidation, in which a larger vendor adds sixty percent of a competing product's functionality, collapsing the renewal economics; and the lowering of switching costs by AI, so that the internal rebuild that previously required months now requires a sprint. Annual deal structures, originally designed to protect retention, are suppressing the signal: buyers who have effectively decided to leave are still appearing in retention data.

Source: OnlyCFO Newsletter, "2026: The Year of Churn," April 2026

A gap is opening between cancellation rate and actual churn. Paying customers who have stopped using a product remain visible in revenue metrics until contract expiry, but in operational terms the commercial relationship has already ended. This pattern, which practitioners are now describing as stealth churn, produces a specific distortion: cancellation data, visible only at renewal, is the lagging confirmation of decisions already made. Survey data published in April 2026 indicates that forty-five percent of new AI budget is being reallocated from existing software line items rather than added to overall technology spend. Vendors reading cancellation rate as a proxy for churn are measuring the moment the relationship formally ends, not the moment it effectively ends.[13]

Cancellation rate and churn rate: not the same number

A practitioner operator publishing in April 2026 draws a distinction that is becoming material at the portfolio level: "If you run a B2B platform and you don't have a dashboard tracking which paying customers haven't logged in for 30, 60, 90 days, you do not actually know your churn rate. You know your cancellation rate. Those are different numbers now." The same account documents paying for a productivity platform months after the team stopped using it, with no cancellation triggered. A survey of CIOs corroborates the mechanism: forty-five percent of new AI budget is drawn from existing software line items, not from incremental spend. Active usage data, API call volumes, and task frequency are the leading indicators of retention health. Cancellation data, visible at renewal, is the lagging confirmation of decisions already made.

Source: SaaStr, "Our Salesforce Bill Went UP 80%, Marketo Can't Honor Its Own Unsubscribes, and We Stealth-Churned Off Notion," April 2026; Redpoint CIO survey, 2026

A new commercial distribution architecture is now visible at the high end, and has moved beyond early signal into confirmed structure. One of the largest foundation model providers has launched a $1.5 billion joint venture, backed by major private equity partners, to acquire enterprise AI services firms and embed engineers directly into customer environments. The rationale given publicly is explicit: enterprises do not need more AI demonstrations; they need people who can integrate AI into existing workflows, data systems, and operations. If this model establishes itself at scale, it represents a structural change in how enterprise AI capability reaches its buyers. The traditional vendor-led procurement cycle is bypassed. The commercial relationship is structured around the delivery of outcomes inside the customer's own environment, not the licensing of tools from outside it.[16]

The transition is also registering in how vendors structure their investor-facing financial disclosures. At least one of the largest enterprise software platforms has reorganised its revenue reporting from a product-line architecture built around cloud application suites and named user access to a structure that foregrounds AI agent capability as a primary commercial unit. This is not a cosmetic change to product labelling. The decision to reorganise financial disclosure around an agent-based architecture is a structural acknowledgement, made at the level of regulatory filings and investor guidance, that the commercial model has changed. When a market-leading platform restructures how it reports to its investors, it is doing something categorically different from launching a product: it is adjusting the unit by which it expects to be measured. This kind of signal belongs to the lagging category, not the leading one. When it appears in formal disclosure, the underlying commercial conviction has already been in motion for some time.[14]

The unit of account is changing

One of the largest enterprise software platforms restructured its revenue reporting from FY27, moving from application-suite categories to two primary units: an AI agent capability layer and a data and platform infrastructure layer. The company stated this aligns with its "AI-first Agentic Enterprise strategy." What matters here is not the product strategy but the reporting decision: the company is formally telling its investors that AI agents, not seats, are the primary unit through which commercial value is now organised. Reorganising investor disclosure to reflect a new commercial architecture is a lagging signal, not a leading one. It confirms, from within one of the largest incumbents in the market, that the transition documented in buyer behaviour throughout this section is now structurally visible on the vendor side as well.

Source: Salesforce Inc., Fiscal Year 2027 Revenue Reporting Update, 30 April 2026; financial press reporting, 1-2 May 2026

The response of the most sophisticated incumbents to the headless transition is not passive. At least one major enterprise workflow platform has released an architecture that proactively enables third-party AI agents to execute processes through governed interfaces, bypassing the user interface layer by design rather than by default. By decoupling execution logic from screen-based interaction, the platform positions itself as the central execution layer for agent workflows rather than as a product that agents route around without oversight. The commercial logic of this move is significant: the incumbent is not defending the interface; it is offering governed access through the interface's absence. Whether this architectural pivot preserves the pricing power that the interface historically enabled is a question the market has not yet answered, but the direction of intent is visible.[17]

Incumbents are building the headless layer themselves

In May 2026, a major enterprise software platform released a headless architecture enabling third-party AI agents to execute workflows via standard agent protocols. The stated rationale was explicit: decouple logic from user interfaces; prioritise data and workflow quality over screen design; position the platform as the central execution layer for agent-driven processes. This is structurally different from the compliance-and-switching-cost defence that characterises most incumbent responses to competitive pressure. It is an architectural acknowledgement that the interface is not the durable asset, combined with a structural claim on the governed infrastructure that persists when the interface is absent. The platform is not being bypassed; it is being repositioned from the surface of work to the substrate of it.

Source: TechZine, "The UI is dead, long live the agent: ServiceNow goes headless and opens its platform," 14 May 2026

The commercial architecture of AI-native pricing requires operational infrastructure that the SaaS era did not build. Subscription billing systems designed for flat-rate seat access cannot natively track, aggregate, and price variable consumption at the granularity that usage- and outcome-based models require. The industry is now making infrastructure investments in response: metering platforms, usage aggregation systems, and real-time pricing engines are moving from third-party integrations to owned infrastructure at the level of major platform vendors. These investments represent a different class of signal from the pricing announcements and investor disclosures that have characterised the transition narrative so far. They are operational bets, made inside the cost structure of large incumbents, on the permanence of the model being described.[21]

Infrastructure follows commitment

One of the largest enterprise software platforms announced in June 2026 the acquisition of a specialist metering and usage-based billing platform designed to track consumption at the granularity required for AI-native pricing: token counts, API calls, resolution events, and outcome verification sequences. The acquisition follows the same company's reorganisation of investor disclosure around an agent-based commercial architecture announced two months earlier. The two decisions are complementary and sequential: first, declare that agents are the commercial unit; then, acquire the operational infrastructure needed to bill for them at scale. Billing infrastructure commitments are structurally significant because they are operationally difficult to reverse. The commercial model transition is now visible not only in what incumbents say about their pricing strategy, but in what they are choosing to own.

Source: Financial Times and technology press, reporting on acquisition of metering platform m3ter, June 2026

A revenue training firm serving more than 600 enterprise go-to-market organisations retired its multi-year licence model in May 2026 and moved to outcome pricing tied to win rate, sales cycle time, and revenue per lead. The firm described the shift in language that maps onto the broader transition: 'pay for what works, outcomes not access.' When the advisers adopt the architecture they have been recommending, it is a different class of signal from when the clients do.

03

The View from Three Years Out

The future of enterprise value is not waiting to be modelled. It is waiting to be perceived. The enterprises that will define commercial norms in three years are already running experiments the rest of the market regards as too complex to scale. They are not too complex. They are too unfamiliar.

A historical precedent makes the direction of this transition legible even before its destination is visible. When a more efficient steam engine made coal-powered energy dramatically cheaper in the late eighteenth century, the intuitive expectation was that demand for coal would fall. What followed was the reverse: consumption expanded across the following century. Cheaper energy had not reduced the use of energy; it had expanded the range of processes it became economical to attempt.

The same dynamic is now structurally visible in AI-assisted knowledge work. As the cost of finding, synthesising, and translating information collapses, the practical consequence is not a reduction in the demand for knowledge work but a redistribution of where value sits within it. When synthesis and retrieval approach near-zero cost, the constraint migrates to what remains scarce: judgment, interpretation, and the capacity to produce something genuinely new from cheap material.

The commercial implication for enterprise software follows directly. Vendors whose value proposition rests on access to information, or on executing processes that information makes possible, are being repriced by the same structural dynamic that repriced energy two centuries ago. The next pricing power sits in the layer where human judgment remains irreducible and cannot be replicated at the same collapsing cost.[18]

Outcome-based pricing, currently confined to large strategic accounts with high data maturity on both sides, will become a standard option in enterprise software negotiations within three years. Shared data environments and agreed measurement frameworks are maturing faster than the commercial frameworks for using them. The constraint is not technical. It is the absence of commercial practitioners who know how to structure, negotiate, and manage outcome-linked contracts at scale.

The unbundling of enterprise platforms will continue, but not uniformly. Vendors with strong claims on identity, workflow, and compliance infrastructure will hold their core positions. The economics of replacing a system of record that sits at the centre of a company's operational data flows are prohibitive for most buyers regardless of the available alternatives. What will not hold is the pricing power that platform vendors have historically extracted from the layers built on top of those cores: the automation tools, the analytics modules, the integration middleware. Those are the positions that AI-native alternatives are targeting, and they will succeed in the accounts where the compliance argument is weakest.

The unbundling dynamic has a further dimension not yet widely visible in commercial strategy. As AI agents acquire the ability to access enterprise platforms programmatically, the interface layer that historically bundled value with usage begins to separate. A buyer may maintain access to the full data and workflow infrastructure of an enterprise platform while never directly interacting with the vendor's interface. The platform becomes headless in practice, accessed entirely through an agent that routes requests, retrieves data, and executes actions invisibly to the user. Where this pattern establishes itself, the vendor retains the data relationship but loses the daily interaction that sustains the perceived value of the product, the upsell opportunity that the interface creates, and the user habit that makes the next renewal straightforward. The commercial logic of this separation has not yet been priced into most enterprise software valuations.[13]

The emerging discipline

Value engineering, currently a support function within enterprise software sales organisations, is on a trajectory toward becoming a core commercial discipline with its own data systems, methodologies, and board visibility. Companies that invest in this capability before their competitors will price more effectively, retain customers more reliably, and expand revenue with less friction. The gap between those who do and those who do not will be visible in net revenue retention figures within two to three years.

The enterprises building toward this frontier are already visible, not in the data that analysts are collecting but in the places analysts are not yet looking: research environments, early developer communities, and the work of practitioners attempting the most demanding version of the value measurement and pricing problem. Academic research typically precedes commercial application by years, often by a decade. The firms that position themselves with structured proximity to where the next generation of AI infrastructure is being built will be able to see where the market is heading before the rest of the market registers the movement. The others will discover the shift when it appears in renewal data.

A structural signal arrived in June 2026 that extends the transition further than this analysis has previously traced. The infrastructure layer for commercial exchange itself is being built to accommodate non-human actors. In the same week, major payment network operators announced dedicated architectures enabling AI agents to initiate, authorise, and complete financial transactions without requiring human instruction at the moment of purchase. The commercial implication is not primarily about payment technology. It is about who the commercial counterparty is anticipated to be. Investment decisions at the infrastructure level of payment networks are made against long planning horizons. The thesis embedded in these announcements is that AI agents will become independent commercial actors at sufficient scale, within the timeframe relevant to infrastructure investment, to justify dedicated network architecture. If that thesis proves correct, the transition described in this publication does not end with outcome-based pricing for human buyers. It extends to a commercial environment in which the buyer itself may be a machine.[22]

The payment rails being built for agents

In a single week in June 2026, two of the largest global payment network operators announced commercial infrastructure for AI-agent-initiated transactions: one, a card platform enabling AI agents to make purchases, verify credentials, and transact autonomously; the other, a partnership with a major AI systems developer enabling models to complete purchases and bookings without human intervention. Both organisations are building for a commercial environment in which AI agents are not assistants to the purchasing decision but participants in it. Payment infrastructure investments at this scale and with this degree of coordination do not describe an anticipated edge case. They describe an anticipated norm. The question this raises for commercial model design is direct: what does a revenue architecture look like when the buyer is an agent running on behalf of an agent, with no human in the decision loop?

Source: Reuters and financial press, reporting on Mastercard Agent Pay for Machines and Visa agentic commerce infrastructure partnership, June 2026

The role of the buyer will also change in ways that most sellers have not yet modelled. As AI-assisted procurement becomes standard, buyers will arrive at commercial conversations with more precise value benchmarks, better alternatives analysis, and a clearer view of their own usage patterns than most vendors currently have. Sellers have historically known what a product is worth to a customer better than the customer does. That advantage is narrowing. Vendors who have built their sales approach around that advantage have less time to adapt than they may assume.

The provocation

The professional instinct in commercial strategy is to resolve, clarify, model, present: to have an answer. The artists and poets who have most clearly perceived what analysts missed did something different: they waited. They held unresolved questions without urgency to close them, and in that waiting, something became visible that urgency prevents. The question is not what the future of enterprise value will look like. It is: what would it mean to gestate a pricing model rather than build one? To live inside the question of value long enough that the answer surfaces?

04

The Case Against

The transition narrative underestimates three things that incumbents understand and their challengers mostly do not: the structural cost of compliance, the depth of integration value, and the institutional logic of large enterprise procurement.

Compliance and auditability are not features sitting alongside the product. For regulated industries, they are the product. The ability to demonstrate to an external auditor, a regulator, or a board exactly how a business process was executed, who authorised each step, and what the system state was at any point in time is worth more to certain buyers than any efficiency gain an alternative might offer. AI-built replacements do not yet carry the compliance and auditability assurance that enterprise procurement in regulated industries requires. Capability is not in question; auditability is. Incumbent platforms in regulated sectors hold positions the transition narrative tends to understate.[4]

Switching costs in mature enterprise software relationships are structural rather than psychological. The integration network of a mature platform represents an investment that is difficult to replicate and costly to abandon: the data flows it manages, the API dependencies built on top of it, the trained user base, and the customisations and configurations accumulated over years. The financial case for replacing a well-embedded system has to be overwhelming before a procurement function will recommend it, and the bar rises with the size and complexity of the organisation. One complication is now visible from practitioner evidence. AI has lowered the cost of routing around a vendor whose product is failing at the margin. Where a specific function is broken or a vendor's rate of change has stalled, a technically literate operator can now replicate or substitute that function faster than the vendor can fix it. The structural lock-in of a well-embedded core system remains real; the protection it extends to individual features and peripheral layers within that system is narrowing.

Two incumbent responses to the headless transition

The transition to headless and agent-mediated access is producing structurally different responses within the incumbent cohort, and both are deliberate. One pattern, already documented in this publication, is proactive: build the headless layer, govern its absence, and reposition the platform as the substrate of work rather than its surface. A second pattern moves in the opposite direction: restrict programmatic access by external AI agents and require customers to route all AI interactions through a proprietary layer. The commercial rationale for the closed strategy is clear. If the proprietary AI interface is the only authorised route to a platform's data, the data position is monetised twice. The early evidence on this bet is cautionary: reported adoption of the proprietary AI layer in at least one major platform pursuing this strategy stands at approximately three percent of the customer base. A lock-in mechanism that has not been adopted is not a lock. The strategy may still succeed as capabilities develop. But where it fails, it will have generated friction and customer resistance without the retention it was designed to produce.

Source: TLDR IT, reporting on divergent incumbent API strategies, 15 May 2026; TechZine, "ServiceNow goes headless and opens its platform," 14 May 2026

A further constraint on the transition is practical rather than structural. Usage-based pricing aligns cost with consumption, but it also makes cost unpredictable in a way that procurement functions are designed to resist. In environments where AI inference costs are variable and potentially unbounded, buyers with large development or operational teams face the risk that a commercial model chosen as a proxy for value becomes an open-ended cost commitment. The organisational response is not to embrace value-aligned pricing but to retreat to the flat-rate predictability that usage-based models were designed to replace. The forces pushing vendors toward consumption pricing are simultaneously pushing some buyers back toward subscription. This is not a transitional friction that resolves as the market matures. It is a structural tension between the vendor's need to recover variable AI infrastructure cost and the buyer's need to control budget. Understanding the model does not resolve it.[24]

Buyer retreat: when variable cost becomes uncontrollable

In June 2026, two pricing decisions in the AI coding tools market illustrated the structural tension from opposite sides of the table. A major technology company withdrew its software engineering teams from a usage-based AI coding tool, citing per-engineer token costs of approximately $2,000 per month, and reassigned them to a flat-rate alternative. In the same period, the platform managing the flat-rate alternative converted every plan to usage-based AI-credit billing. The vendor is moving toward consumption pricing to recover variable AI infrastructure cost; the buyer is moving away from it to protect budget predictability. Both decisions are rational responses to the same underlying dynamic: in an AI-native product, the marginal cost of intensive use is real. The pricing model can shift who holds the variable cost exposure, but it cannot eliminate the exposure. Buyers who discover this at scale, as AI usage intensifies inside their organisations, will negotiate toward the model that protects budget control, regardless of its alignment with value delivered. The transition to value-based pricing does not face only institutional resistance. It faces a genuine cost arithmetic problem for buyers whose usage patterns generate costs that flat-rate pricing was designed to make invisible.

Source: TLDR Founders, "AI Coding Tools Are Quietly Re-Pricing Themselves," reporting on seat economics in AI developer tooling, June 2026[24]

The buying process in large enterprises is designed around a goal that is not value optimisation. It is risk elimination. The vendor that wins a large account is often not the one with the best product or the most favourable commercial model. It is the one that presents the smallest institutional risk to the person signing the contract. Until the risk profile of newer commercial models and newer vendors is well-established across multiple comparable deployments, the incumbents' position in large enterprise is protected by something more durable than product quality or pricing. It is protected by the structure of the buying decision itself.

A fourth constraint is organisational capability rather than institutional structure. Survey analysis based on interactions with more than 3,800 technology executives globally finds that only seventeen percent of enterprises are operating at the level of AI maturity required to execute and scale high-value AI use cases. High-maturity organisations deploy AI initiatives at twice the rate of medium-maturity organisations and more than five times the rate of low-maturity ones. The gap is not primarily technological. It is the absence of governance infrastructure, decision-making architecture, and AI literacy needed to translate experimentation into operational value at scale.[7]

What CIOs are reporting

"Every team has their own AI pilots, but we still don't have a coherent way to run the business with AI at the center. We're stuck in use-case mode." This observation, from a CIO in financial services, reflects a pattern appearing consistently across industries. The transition to value-based commercial models requires buyers capable of measuring, governing, and demonstrating value with enough precision to anchor an outcome-linked contract. Where the organisational infrastructure for that does not yet exist, the transition stalls regardless of what is available in the market. The 83% of enterprises not yet at high AI maturity represent both the eventual scale of the opportunity and the structural reason it will take longer to arrive.

Source: Gartner, "AI Maturity Depends on More than Technology Capability: CIO Perspectives," March 2026 (G00851188)
What this means for the thesis

The transition to value-based commercial models will not be uniform across the market. It will be fastest in the segments with the lowest compliance burden, the shortest deployment history, and the most sophisticated buyers. It will be slowest in exactly the accounts that matter most to the incumbents' revenue base. Both things can be true simultaneously: the model is changing, and the incumbents have more time than the disruption narrative implies.

A fifth counterargument sits in the market data itself. Analysis of enterprise software spending patterns as of early 2026 finds that the vast majority of spend remains anchored in seat-based models, and that consumption-based revenue has barely moved, despite the consistent expectation among market commentators that the shift was imminent. The transition narrative is currently running ahead of buyer behaviour. The gap is not evidence that the direction is wrong. It is a measure of the distance between the moment of perception and the moment of practice: between the leading practitioners who have demonstrated the new model and the institutional majority who have not yet assembled the conditions to replicate it.[11]
